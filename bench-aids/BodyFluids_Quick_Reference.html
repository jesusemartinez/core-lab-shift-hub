✓ Classification

Light's criteria separate exudate from transudate in pleural/peritoneal fluids

Transudate vs Exudate — Light's Criteria
Apply to pleural, peritoneal, and pericardial fluids
An exudate meets ≥1 of Light's criteria; a transudate meets none. Meeting criteria is ~98% sensitive for exudate. Transudates = hydrostatic/oncotic pressure imbalances; Exudates = inflammation/infection/malignancy.
Light's Criteria (ANY ONE = Exudate)
① Fluid/Serum protein ratio > 0.5
② Fluid/Serum LDH ratio > 0.6
③ Fluid LDH > ⅔ upper limit of normal serum LDH

CSF

Cerebrospinal fluid — normal and abnormal findings

Normal CSF
Crystal clear, colorless — <5 WBC/µL, protein 15–45 mg/dL
Normal CSF is water-clear and colorless, like water. Contains only lymphocytes and monocytes (<5/µL total). Protein 15–45 mg/dL; glucose >60% of serum glucose. No RBCs, no neutrophils, no organisms.
Appearance: Clear/Colorless WBC: <5/µL (Lymph/Mono) Protein: 15–45 mg/dL Glucose: >60% serum
Xanthochromia
Yellow/orange-tinged CSF — old blood or high protein
Yellow or orange discoloration from oxyhemoglobin (RBC breakdown) or bilirubin. Begins 2–4 hours after subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH), peaks at 12 hours, persists 1–2 weeks. Also: high protein (>150 mg/dL), jaundice, severe hyperbilirubinemia. Key test to distinguish true SAH from traumatic tap.
SAH — most important use Traumatic tap: xanthochromia absent
Xanthochromia present → rule out SAH; CT + LP
Bacterial Meningitis
Many neutrophils (PMNs) — turbid CSF
Classic triad: ↑↑ WBC with PMN predominance, ↓glucose (<45 mg/dL), ↑protein (>100 mg/dL). Turbid/cloudy appearance. Organisms on Gram stain (positive in 60–90%). Send culture immediately. Treat empirically while awaiting results.
WBC >1,000/µL (PMN) Glucose <45 mg/dL Protein >100 mg/dL
Urgent: Gram stain + culture + sensitivity

Pleural

Pleural fluid — thoracentesis specimen analysis

Mesothelial Cells
Expected lining cells of pleural, pericardial, peritoneal cavities
Round to polygonal cells with abundant cytoplasm (often appears "two-toned"). Central round nucleus with prominent nucleolus. May appear in reactive clusters. Normal finding in fluid specimens. Absent in TB pleuritis (blocked by fibrin) — absence raises suspicion for TB.
Normal lining cells — expected Absent in TB pleuritis
Malignant Cells
Large atypical cells, clusters, prominent nucleoli
Large cells with high N:C ratio, irregular/enlarged nuclei, prominent nucleoli, and coarse chromatin. May form clusters or acinar/glandular arrangements (adenocarcinoma). Most common: lung adenocarcinoma, breast CA, lymphoma, mesothelioma. Send cell block for immunostains.
High N:C ratio Irregular nuclei / nucleoli Send cell block for immunostains
Suspicious/Positive for malignancy → pathology review
Synovial

Joint fluid — arthrocentesis analysis and crystal identification

Normal Synovial Fluid
Viscous, clear/pale yellow, <200 WBC, few mononuclear cells
Normal synovial fluid is viscous (high molecular weight hyaluronate), pale yellow, and clear. The string sign: normal fluid strings 4–6 cm before breaking. Non-inflammatory fluid has <200 WBC/µL with predominantly mononuclear cells and no crystals.
WBC: <200/µL PMN: <25% Viscosity: High String: 4–6 cm
Septic Arthritis
Purulent synovial fluid — gross example
Gross purulent/turbid synovial fluid example. Correlate with WBC count, differential, Gram stain, crystals, and culture; >50,000/µL with PMN predominance strongly supports septic arthritis.
WBC >50,000 → suspect septic >90% PMN Gram stain + culture STAT
Urgent: Culture + sensitivities; orthopedic consult
Crystals

Crystal arthropathies — polarized microscopy is essential for identification

Monosodium Urate (MSU) — Gout
Needle-shaped; negative birefringent (yellow parallel to slow axis)
Long, thin, needle-shaped crystals, often intracellular (within neutrophils). Under compensated polarized light: yellow when parallel to slow axis = negative birefringence. Classic: big toe (podagra), ankle, knee. Uric acid >6.8 mg/dL in blood. Not always elevated during acute attack.
Gout · Negative birefringent · Yellow parallel
CPPD — Pseudogout
Rhomboid/rod-shaped; positive birefringent (blue parallel)
Calcium pyrophosphate dihydrate. Short, rhomboid or rod-shaped crystals, often intracellular. Under polarized light: blue when parallel to slow axis = positive (weak) birefringence. Knee is most common joint. Associated with aging, hyperparathyroidism, hemochromatosis, hypomagnesemia.
Pseudogout · Positive birefringent · Blue parallel

Crystal Birefringence Quick Guide
Compensated polarized light microscopy
Negative birefringence (MSU/gout): Crystal yellow when parallel to slow axis of compensator. Positive birefringence (CPPD): Crystal blue when parallel. Use a first-order red compensator and slow axis indicator on your microscope.
Peritoneal

Ascitic fluid — paracentesis specimen analysis

Normal Peritoneal / Ascitic Fluid
Clear to straw-colored; mesothelial cells + sparse mononuclear cells
Use this card as a gross/color reference only. Normal peritoneal fluid is sparse, clear to straw-colored, and typically contains mesothelial cells with scattered mononuclear cells. SAAG remains the key classification test for ascites.
SAAG (Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient)
SAAG = Serum Albumin − Ascites Albumin
≥1.1 g/dL → Portal HTN (cirrhosis, CHF)
<1.1 g/dL → Non-portal (malignancy, TB, pancreatitis)
Malignant Ascites
Atypical cell clusters; acinar arrangements; high N:C ratio
Malignant cells in ascites are often arranged in three-dimensional clusters, acini, or glandular formations (adenocarcinoma). Common primaries: ovarian, colorectal, gastric, pancreatic, breast. High N:C ratio, irregular/prominent nucleoli, coarse chromatin. SBP (Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis): PMN >250/µL = diagnostic, even without positive culture.
Acinar clusters → adenocarcinoma PMN >250/µL → SBP Cell block + immunostains for primary
PMN >250/µL: Treat empirically for SBP; blood + ascites cultures
